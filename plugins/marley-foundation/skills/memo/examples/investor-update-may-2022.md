## tl;dr

On April 21, we launched our first fully functional virtual primary care clinic in Wisconsin. We have now built the capabilities needed to offer virtual care products and services. We will add Illinois and Michigan shortly, and then Minnesota, Nebraska and Missouri by August 2022. Our goal is to create a Midwest regional pod, with sufficient reach, that can be served by the same clinical team, same clinical protocols and the same marketing campaigns. We will use this first region to iterate and learn, and improve our patient acquisition, our clinical offering and our patient retention.

Overall, we are very happy with how the team is coming together and how it is performing. We have a great initial team that is almost complete, and we think this team can carry us for a while with no additional hires.

We have not yet figured out patient acquisition, but are steadily learning, and we now have a more complete product to try to sell. Patient acquisition for these populations is a hard problem that will take a lot of tenacity, a good bit of creativity and some luck, but it is a worthy and fun problem to solve. It feels like a pretty good time to just hunker down and figure some things out, then re-emerge with all these great learnings when it's a little warmer out. We are starting to think a lot about cash positions and funding scenarios, and how to best survive winter and come out strong.

## Launch of V2 Offering

On April 21, about 10 months from funding, we launched our first fully functional virtual primary care clinic in Wisconsin. We launched approximately 2.5 weeks later than our initial goal, due to some unforeseen legal and insurance issues, but we feel good about completing the V2.0 offering. This V2.0 offering has all the functional capabilities needed to create and market virtual care products. In the process, we learned how to launch in a state, and now have a good checklist for the rollout of new states.

**Components of current offering:** Licensed care team (that is great), EMR, Patient↔care team messaging, eRx to local pharmacy, eRx to home delivery (coming soon), Hypertension clinical protocol + CPAs, Clinical escalation protocols, Self-scheduling, Reminders, Customer support, Phone and Video visits, Remote BP devices, Local labs, **Lots of duct tape.**

Built to be compliant for Medicare billing (as well as commercial). It is functional, but neither pretty nor delightful. This is our next priority.

## Patient Acquisition (excerpt)

We were pretty unsuccessful with our attempts to get patients to schedule Zoom visits and show up. There were a lot of potential challenges with this flow, including: bad reminders, bad handoffs to partners, downloading Zoom, requires patient to take action, etc.

We decided it would be very challenging to debug this flow due to the number of possible break points, and we decided to change to a simpler approach. Our new approach converts leads to scheduled phone calls, which allows us to reach out to them proactively, at the scheduled time.

## What to learn from this example

- **Headline first.** The tl;dr opens with the dated launch event — concrete and specific.
- **Honest about timing slippage.** "We launched approximately 2.5 weeks later than our initial goal, due to some unforeseen legal and insurance issues." Not buried, not spun.
- **"Lots of duct tape"** as an explicit line item in the V2 components list. Characteristic Marley honesty.
- **"It is functional, but neither pretty nor delightful."** Direct self-assessment without false-modesty or overstating.
- **Help Requests are specific** — three named profiles for the VP/Dir Marketing role; named markets for intros; named topic ("unique partnerships").
- **Fundraising questions are numbered and genuinely open** — these are not rhetorical; they invite investor input on real decisions.
- **"It feels like a pretty good time to just hunker down and figure some things out, then re-emerge with all these great learnings when it's a little warmer out."** — colloquial warmth in a paragraph about runway and macro conditions. This is the voice.

# Market, ICP, and Competitive Landscape

## The big market

- **108 million Americans / 45% of US adults** have high blood pressure (defined as average BP > 130/80, per the 2017 guideline change).
- **80 million Americans / 29% of US adults** under the pre-2017 definition (BP > 140/90).
- **~50%** of hypertension patients are already diagnosed and medically treated.
- **Only 1 in 4** people with hypertension are at goal in the US.
- **63% prevalence** among people over 60 years old.
- Hypertension accounts for **54% of all strokes** and **47% of all ischemic heart disease** globally.
- Patients with HTN cost ~**$9,000/year**, with ~$2,000 attributable to hypertension itself. Compared to non-HTN patients: **2.5x inpatient costs, 2x outpatient, 3x pharmacy.**

## Marley's TAM logic

From the founding concept doc:
- ~80M Americans with HTN
- ~53M are "uncomplicated" (no CKD, CHF, prior MI)
- At least **27M (50% of uncomplicated)** are not at BP goal — this is Marley's first-order TAM

Treatment guidelines for HTN are clear: five classes of medicines, at least one good generic in each. Most patients are on multiple medicines with varying doses; it often takes multiple medication changes over too long a period to reach the right configuration. **This is the opportunity:** the system has the tools; it just doesn't use them well.

## ICP — Marley's target patient

### Demographics
- **Age 50–75**, with the sweet spot likely 55–70.
- Both men and women, expected to lean slightly female (women more likely to search for health information and take action online — Propeller experience).
- Has comorbidities, but **no kidney disease, CHF, or prior MI** (excluded from initial cohort).
- Diagnosed with hypertension, on medication, but **not at BP goal**.

### Psychographics
- "Dissatisfied with their current care plans or care teams and are seeking a better solution and better results." (Press-release doc)
- Has tried lifestyle change but struggles with consistency.
- Has at least one bp cuff at home, but may not be actively using it.
- Open to virtual care (some are; not all — see Marley patient research for the nuance).
- Motivated by family ("wanting to be there for family and friends") and/or fear of adverse events (especially stroke).

### Goals (from Marley patient research, Jan 2022)
Patients fall into one of three goal patterns:
1. Staying in range (or getting to goal, if not there yet)
2. Improving lifestyle, usually eating better
3. **Getting off medication entirely** (substantial latent demand — under-served by the current system)

### What Marley patients actually look like (Nov 2022 cohort, 55 enrolled patients)
- **Mean age 59.5**, range 40–82, **33% aged 65+**
- **Mean 4.9 chronic conditions** (range 1–11) — complex co-morbid population
- **57%** have anxiety, depression, or other mental health diagnosis
- **86%** are overweight or obese; **65%** have clinical-level obesity
- **84%** have existing PCPs
- SDOH: meaningful subset on disability or experiencing financial stress; some are truck drivers; some have history of childhood abuse/trauma

This complexity is **intentional** — Marley wants to serve the real chronic-disease population, not a healthy subset.

## Unmet needs (from patient research)

From the Jan 2022 UserInterview research with 10 hypertension patients, four unmet needs surfaced:

1. **Understanding the "root cause"** of their BP fluctuations and how to manage them
2. **Getting to goal and then reducing** the number and dose of their medications
3. **Managing stress and anxiety**, especially relating to hypertension "what-ifs"
4. **Access to a hypertension expert** (more than a PCP) who prioritizes their personal goals

Patients in research were **most excited about**: the BP cuff that automatically shares readings + trends. Multiple were already doing this manually (one had built an Excel spreadsheet).

Patients were **most skeptical about**: the virtual care team (those with prior bad telehealth experiences). Marley's job is to prove the care team valuable through proactivity, personal touch, and consistent relationships.

## Competitive landscape

### Direct competitors
- **Hello Heart** — consumer BP-tracking app; not full care delivery.
- **Heartbeat Health** — CV/HTN-focused, broader CV.
- **Livongo / Teladoc** — HTN as an add-on to their diabetes model, gated by employer/payer contracts.
- **Omada** — HTN as an add-on to behavioral-health-first model; same B2B gating.

### Adjacent / structural competitors
- **One Medical, Galileo, Firefly** — broad-population virtual primary care. Don't specialize in chronic disease.
- **Oak Street, ChenMed, Iora** — full-risk Medicare PCP. Brick-and-mortar; not virtual.
- **Ro, Hims, Nurx** — DTC Rx for sensitive/specific conditions; not chronic-disease management.

### How Marley wins against each
- Vs. Hello Heart / Heartbeat: full care delivery, not just monitoring. Real prescriptions, real outcomes.
- Vs. Livongo / Omada: direct acquisition (B2C2B), not employer-gated. Broader brand reach.
- Vs. One Medical / Galileo / Firefly: specialization in chronic disease segments; better outcomes within those segments.
- Vs. Oak / ChenMed: virtual-first, scalable nationally, lower per-patient operating cost.
- Vs. Ro / Hims: ongoing chronic-disease care vs. transactional Rx; in-network with payers, not cash-only.

## Geographic strategy

Marley launches state-by-state, building **regional "pods"** of contiguous states served by a single clinical team with shared protocols and marketing.

Initial pod: **Midwest** — Wisconsin (launched April 2022), Illinois, Michigan, then Minnesota, Nebraska, Missouri, Ohio.

State-selection criteria: **value (patient volume × payer concentration) × effort (timeline × operational lift)**.

## Payer landscape strategy

Marley's network contracting target (per Nov 2022 update): contract with **all major payers** (commercial, Medicare FFS, MA, state Medicaids, MCOs) in each active state to achieve **80%+ coverage per state**. Estimated 33 network contracts across the initial 5 states; CertifyOS is the credentialing partner.

Long-term: move from FFS billing → PMPM contracts → full risk capitation as evidence and scale support it. Full capitation for this population modeled in the **$1,000–$1,500 PMPM range** (Oak/Iora benchmarks).

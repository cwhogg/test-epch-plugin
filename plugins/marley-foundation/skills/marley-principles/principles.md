# Marley Medical Principles

These are the operating principles that shape how Marley Medical decides what to build, how to build it, and how to win. They are drawn from the founding concept doc (June 2020), the Series Seed narrative (May 2021), and the patterns in how the company has actually executed since.

When making decisions about strategy, product, or positioning, return to these principles before reaching for outside frameworks.

---

## 1. Plans are hypotheses, not commitments.

Quoting Eisenhower: *"Plans are useless, but planning is indispensable."* Quoting Tyson: *"Everyone has a plan until they get punched in the mouth."* Marley wins by **planning, learning, iterating, and adapting faster than others in a large market** — not by clinging to a fixed roadmap.

Every plan is our current best guess and set of hypotheses. We commit to direction, not to specific tactics, until evidence tells us what's actually working.

## 2. We're building virtual-first primary care for *specific patient segments* — not for everyone.

"Specialized primary care." We pick patient segments with chronic disease that fit our model — uncomplicated hypertension first, then high cholesterol, then orally-treated type 2 diabetes — and build a best-in-class experience around each segment. We treat the whole patient, but we acquire and target by segment.

This is the core of how Marley differs from broad-population primary care (Galileo, ONEM) and from single-indication digital health (Livongo, Omada).

## 3. B2C2B, not B2B2C.

We acquire patients directly via online and offline marketing, then use that patient base as leverage in payer negotiations. We are not gated by a B2B intermediary for growth.

Why: at Propeller we lived in the B2B2C world — marketing material delayed by payer compliance, growth capped by payer list size, modern marketing techniques restricted by contract geography. With Marley, we own the relationship with the patient first, then bring contracts in around it.

## 4. Build the brand patients want to pick.

As care moves virtual, brand becomes the dominant factor in patient selection — replacing proximity. We build a brand that's modern, warm, witty, and inspires confidence — see [[voice]] for how that sounds — because patients, physicians, and payers will increasingly use trusted brands for managing more diseases over time.

## 5. Shockingly enjoyable.

Our goal is a patient experience that "rivals any consumer experience on the market" — an experience more like Apple than Kaiser Permanente. "Shockingly enjoyable" is the phrase from the founding doc and it's the bar.

Chronic-disease management should not feel like work. Frictionless is the floor; delightful is the goal.

## 6. Fast feedback loops beat episodic care.

Today's care model is episodic check-ins separated by months. Ours is continuous monitoring + rapid iteration of treatment until the patient is at goal. We measure, predict, adjust, and re-measure — faster than the system currently does, and getting faster as our data grows.

The clinical north star: get patients to BP goal as quickly as possible (escalation mode), then keep them at goal (maintenance mode).

## 7. We use existing tools — we just use them well.

The tools to manage chronic disease already exist: generic medicines, connected devices, telemedicine, coaches, established guidelines. The system uses them poorly. Marley's job is to **integrate** them into a seamless end-to-end experience, not to invent something pharmacologically novel.

This shapes build-vs-buy: ship fast by partnering and using off-the-shelf products; vertically integrate over time where it improves the experience or the economics.

## 8. Broad in-network coverage, multi-plan.

We contract as an in-network provider with as many commercial and government payers as possible — not a narrow B2B carve-out. Patients should not be excluded from Marley because of who their insurance is. This is what enables the B2C2B flow at scale.

## 9. The whole patient — not just the disease.

We acquire patients by segment, but we treat the whole patient. We do not run a single-disease product that hands the patient back to the system for everything else. We are their primary care.

## 10. Data is defensibility.

Treatment of chronic disease is currently more art than science — significant variability, often arbitrary decisions. The dataset Marley builds — different treatment paths and outcomes for patients in our population — is unique, valuable, and gets better over time. We turn art into science, and that becomes a moat.

## 11. Eliminate friction that disincentivizes good behavior.

Co-pays for actions we want patients to take (filling meds, attending visits, getting cuffs) are friction that punishes the behavior we're trying to encourage. Wherever benefit design and law allow, we reduce or remove these for our patients.

## 12. Honest, plainspoken communication — internally and externally.

This is the voice principle. We don't dress up bad news, we don't inflate good news, and we don't hide behind jargon. See [[voice]] for the full treatment.

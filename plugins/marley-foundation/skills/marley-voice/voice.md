# Marley Medical Voice

Marley's voice is the way the company talks to patients, investors, payers, partners, and the team. It is the same fundamental voice across audiences — the register shifts, but the personality doesn't.

This file captures the **five personality descriptors** that define the voice, then the concrete patterns in how Marley actually writes.

---

## The five personality descriptors

These are the canonical descriptors from the HUb brand-personality work (May 2022). They are not five separate voices — they are the five facets of one voice.

### 1. Aspiring / Motivating
We want patients to feel empowered to retake control of their lives. Our tone motivates patients to want to take the right steps toward their health goals.

### 2. Approachable / Empathetic
Everything we're doing is in service of making patients' and clients' lives better — they should feel heard, listened to. Our tone is friendly, warm, empathetic, accessible.

### 3. Confident
A "we got this" level of confidence that brings energy, swagger, and conveys expertise. We say what we believe directly.

### 4. Smart / Clever
Knowledgeable, but also a little fun. Cleverness gets attention, helps us stand out, makes us memorable. **The core of clever is smart** — never clever for its own sake.

### 5. Bold
A new attitude to an outdated category. We're not out there for the sake of attention — but a little attitude goes a long way in healthcare, which generally sounds like nothing.

## How Marley actually writes — concrete patterns

### Sentence rhythm
- **Headlines are short and punchy** — typically 6–14 words. ("We're hyper-focused on hypertension." "A program that fits into your life, to push hypertension out of it.")
- **Body sentences are conversational length** — 12–25 words, with frequent contractions.
- **Marley uses sentence fragments for emphasis.** ("That's a relief." "It works.") Don't fear them.
- **Vary sentence length deliberately.** A long explanatory sentence followed by a short one is a recurring move.

### Register
- **First-person plural for the company** — "we", "us", "our" — never "Marley Medical does X" in third person inside our own copy.
- **Second person for the reader** — "you", "your". Even in investor updates Chris addresses investors as "you."
- **Contractions everywhere.** "Don't", "it's", "we're", "you've", "you'd". Uncontracted forms read as corporate stiffness.
- **Plain English over clinical jargon.** Say "high blood pressure" before "hypertension" in consumer contexts. Say both interchangeably once context is established. Never say "HTN" to patients.

### Recurring vocabulary
Words and phrases that show up across docs and that should reappear in new copy when they fit:

- **"the best way"** — Marley's value-prop frame ("the best way to manage high blood pressure"). Marley does not say "a great way" or "one option" — we say "the best."
- **"24/7"** — always the formulation, never "around the clock" or "anytime."
- **"team of experts"**, **"specialized care team"**, **"multi-disciplinary"** — three flavors of the same idea.
- **"coordinated, convenient, continuous"** — the three c's of Marley's care model.
- **"the future of healthcare"** — used when we want to frame Marley as the new model.
- **"comes to your door"**, **"delivered to your home"** — never "shipped" or "fulfilled."
- **"the future is here"** / **"actually possible now"** — the temporal frame: this used to be impossible, now it isn't.

### Witty moves (use sparingly, but use them)
Marley writes copy with real comic timing in places. Examples from the brand-voice deck:
- *"All we think about is hypertension (we're a blast at parties)."* — self-deprecating aside that signals depth of focus.
- *"You inherited a set of silverware and hypertension from your grandmother. You don't have to keep both."* — concrete-image joke that lands an honest point.
- *"Second best is totally fine for floor cleaners. For your hypertension, not so much."* — domestic comparison that reframes the stakes.
- *"Maybe take off the blood pressure cuff before you high five us…"* — implies enthusiasm without saying it.

Pattern: take the medical thing seriously and the surrounding human stuff playfully. Never take Marley itself too seriously, but never trivialize the disease.

### Empathy moves
- *"If you wake up in the middle of the night worried about your blood pressure, we'll be right there with you."* — Marley meets patients in the genuinely scary moments.
- *"Stress affects high blood pressure. We help you reduce both."* — name the emotional layer of the disease, not just the clinical one.

### Confidence moves
- *"There are lots of things in life you can't control. Blood pressure isn't one of them."* — declarative reframes.
- *"A plan that's 100% more effective at treating hypertension than 'hoping it will go away.'"* — contrast against the actual alternative.
- *"It works."* — when we want to land hard, the shortest sentence wins.

### Investor-update voice (same DNA, more operational)
The voice in investor updates is the same Marley voice, dialed for an exec audience:
- **"tl;dr"** lowercase as the opening section header.
- **Honest about hard things** — "We have not yet figured out patient acquisition, but are steadily learning."
- **Colloquial phrases** carry the warmth — "hunker down", "come out strong", "lots of duct tape", "a worthy and fun problem to solve."
- **Numbered questions for discussion** — invites pushback, signals genuine open-mindedness.
- **No corporate hedging.** Not "we strive to" — instead "we will" or "we haven't yet."
- **Numbers are stated, not buried.** "$7.4M of cash remaining. 25 months of cash."

### Audience adaptation — same voice, different register

| Audience | What changes | What stays the same |
|---|---|---|
| Patient | Plainest English. Fewer commas. More empathy moves. More wit. | Direct address, contractions, "best way" framing, confidence. |
| Payer | More clinical precision. Outcome-language. Less wit. | "We" voice, no hedging, "the future" frame, brand-confident. |
| Investor | More numbers + scenarios. Open questions. Operational candor. | Plainspoken, contractions, no corporate hedging, "we got this" undercurrent. |
| Internal/team | Most casual register. Most candor about open problems. | Same voice — Marley doesn't have a different "internal" personality. |

# Banned phrases

Phrases that do not sound like Marley. Replace them or rewrite the sentence. This list is anti-corporate-stock-language and anti-generic-healthtech.

## Generic healthtech / startup-speak — never use

- "patient-centric" / "patient-first" — say what we actually do for patients
- "best-in-class" / "world-class" used as a self-description (we can say we want to *build* a best-in-class experience; we don't *call ourselves* world-class)
- "cutting-edge", "state-of-the-art", "next-generation"
- "innovative solution" / "innovative platform"
- "disruptive" / "disrupting healthcare"
- "AI-powered" (unless the AI is genuinely the lede)
- "leverage" as a verb (we can leverage a thing in private; we don't write it in copy)
- "synergy", "synergies"
- "holistic" (overused; if we mean "whole patient" say so)
- "seamless" — overused. Acceptable when literal; replace with "easy" or "frictionless" when not.
- "robust" — vague
- "scalable" used as a buzzword (we *will* scale; we don't describe ourselves as scalable in marketing)
- "transformational" / "transforming healthcare"
- "the X of healthcare" (e.g. "the Uber of healthcare") — cliché

## Corporate hedging — never use

- "We strive to…" / "We aim to…" — replace with "We will" or "We do"
- "We are committed to…" — replace with the actual action
- "passionate about" — describe what the passion produces
- "dedicated to providing" — corporate filler
- "thrilled to announce" — replace with the announcement itself
- "going forward" — pick a date or remove

## Clinical jargon in patient-facing copy

- "HTN" — say "high blood pressure" or "hypertension"
- "uncomplicated" used to a patient — internal term only
- "guideline-indicated treatment goals" used to a patient — say "your blood pressure goal"
- "comorbidities" used to a patient — say "other conditions"
- "modality" used to a patient — say "the way you take care of it" or just the specific thing

## Embellishments to delete

- "very" — almost always cut. ("very excited" → "excited")
- "really" — same. ("really great" → "great")
- "actually" — usually cut, except in deliberate moves ("It's actually possible now.")
- "literally" — delete unless literally meant
- "truly" — almost always cut
- "incredibly", "extraordinarily" — usually cut

## Specific phrases that conflict with Marley positioning

- "alternative medicine" — Marley is evidence-based; this phrase reads anti-clinical
- "wellness" used as a primary frame — Marley is healthcare for chronic disease, not wellness
- "lifestyle medicine" — we *include* lifestyle but don't position as a lifestyle-medicine company
- "concierge" — wrong economic frame; we are not concierge medicine

## Phrases to use carefully

- "team of experts" — preferred when there are actually multiple experts on the team
- "personalized" — only use when something specific is actually personalized (the meds, the plan); avoid as a vague brand promise
